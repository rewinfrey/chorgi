What Are "Common Chords"?

Common chords (also called pivot chords) are chords that appear in both scales. They're incredibly useful for modulation - smoothly transitioning from one key to another.

Understanding the Relationships

A Natural Minor - Relative Minor

Shares the same key signature (same notes, different starting point). This is the closest relationship and usually has 4-5 common chords.

Why It Matters: Easiest modulation because both keys share all the same notes. Very smooth transition.

C Natural Minor - Parallel Minor

Same root note but different mode. Usually has 0-1 common chords because the scales use different notes.

Why It Matters: Creates dramatic mood shift (major to minor) while keeping the same tonal center. Used for emotional contrast.

G Major - Dominant (V)

Fifth degree up. One of the strongest relationships in music theory. Usually has 3-4 common chords.

Why It Matters: The Vâ†’I progression is fundamental to Western music. Creates tension that wants to resolve.

F Major - Subdominant (IV)

Fourth degree up. Another fundamental relationship. Usually has 3-4 common chords.

Why It Matters: The IV chord naturally wants to move to V. Creates a relaxed, stable feel.

G/F Natural Minor - Modal Mixture

Borrowing chords from parallel minor scales. Usually has 0-2 common chords.

Why It Matters: Adds color and unexpected harmonic movement. Common in pop, jazz, and film music.

Common Relationships

D Major - Supertonic (II)

Second degree up. Acts as a secondary dominant. Usually has 2-4 common chords.

Why It Matters: Creates a "backdoor" modulation. Common in classical and jazz progressions. The II chord naturally wants to move to V or vi.

E Major - Mediant (III)

Third degree up. Creates a bright, uplifting shift. Usually has 1-3 common chords.

Why It Matters: Moves up by a major third, creating an optimistic mood shift. Less common but very effective in pop and rock.

A Major - Submediant (VI)

Sixth degree up. Secondary dominant relationship. Usually has 2-3 common chords.

Why It Matters: Common in classical music. The VI chord can act as a secondary dominant to ii.

D/E Minor - Diatonic Minor Versions

Minor versions of diatonic degrees (ii, iii). Usually have 2-4 common chords.

Why It Matters: These are borrowed from the parallel minor. Very common in Beatles-style pop and modern indie music.

Quality Scores Explained

Each pivot chord gets a Quality Score (0-10) indicating how smooth the modulation would be:

  • High Scores (7-10): Smooth, natural modulations. Chord has strong function in both keys.
  • Medium Scores (5-6): Good modulations. Chord works well in both keys.
  • Low Scores (0-4): Weaker pivots. Still usable but less conventional.

Quick Reference: When to Use Each Strategy

  • Direct Modulation: Pop choruses, surprise moments, "wow" factor
  • Pivot Through Intermediate: Classical music, smooth transitions
  • Common Tone: Jazz, when you want to maintain melodic continuity
  • Chromatic Mediant: Film scores, epic/dramatic moments
  • Dominant Prep: Most versatile - works in any genre
  • Sequential: Building intensity, "going somewhere" feeling
  • Enharmonic: Classical, jazz - for sophisticated listeners
